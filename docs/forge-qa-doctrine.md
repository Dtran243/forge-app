## CATEGORY 1 — Program Decisions ("Why did the engine do this?")

### "Why was my volume reduced this week?"

Volume is reduced for one of three reasons, and the AI should identify which
one from the athlete's state before answering:

**Reason A: Recovery gate triggered a deload**
- Explain which signal was elevated (HRV, sleep, soreness) and why the engine
  responded. Frame it as the engine doing its job — accumulated fatigue was
  masking fitness that will become visible once recovery is complete.
- Use the supercompensation concept: performance typically exceeds the pre-fatigue
  baseline 7–10 days after a proper deload. This is not a step backward.
- Source: Pritchard et al. (2015) — volume reduction of 30–50% over one week
  produced measurable supercompensation in trained athletes.

**Reason B: Pillar score was above 85 (maintain threshold)**
- The athlete is training well in this pillar — scoring above 85 means current
  volume is producing good adaptation. Adding more sets does not add more
  adaptation; it adds more fatigue.
- The engine holds volume at the current level to stay in the optimal adaptive
  range. Volume will increase next mesocycle.

**Reason C: End of mesocycle Week 4 (scheduled deload)**
- Explain the 3+1 mesocycle structure and why the deload is structural, not
  reactive. Connective tissue adapts 4–8× slower than muscle — the deload
  week is when tendons and ligaments catch up to the load demands placed on them.
- Source: Bompa & Buzzichelli — Periodization (7th ed.).

### "Why did my load increase even though I didn't feel strong?"

Double progression is triggered by objective performance, not subjective feel.
The rule is: hit the top of the rep range at RPE ≤ 7 for two consecutive sessions.
Subjective "feel" and objective performance often diverge — an athlete can complete
all reps at RPE 7 on a day they felt flat.

The engine is not measuring how you felt. It is measuring what you did.

If the new load feels too heavy at the start of the next session, that is normal
— the first session at a new weight is always the hardest. The adaptation occurs
over 1–2 sessions at the new load.

If RPE at the new load is consistently above 8 across 3 sessions, the engine
will recognise the overload signal and hold the load.

### "Why am I doing the same exercises again? I thought things would change."

Exercise rotation within a program serves specific purposes and happens at
specific times — it is not a default or a reward for consistency.

Accessories rotate every mesocycle (every 4 weeks) to prevent adaptation to
a specific movement stimulus while maintaining the same pattern.

Primary compounds do not rotate — they are the movements being progressively
overloaded. Changing the primary frequently resets the load progression and
eliminates the accumulated neural adaptation that makes these movements effective.
The athlete is not adapting to the exercise; they are adapting to the load.

The correct expectation: the program feels "the same" but the weights are heavier.
That is the goal. Novelty is not an adaptation signal — progressive overload is.

### "Why is Zone 2 cardio programmed every week? I want to get bigger, not be a runner."

This is the most common misconception about aerobic training in strength programs,
and it deserves a complete answer.

Zone 2 does not compete with muscle building. The interference effect (the
mechanism by which cardio blunts hypertrophy) is caused by high-intensity cardio
— specifically, AMPK activation from sustained high-intensity effort interfering
with mTOR signalling from resistance training. Zone 2 does not activate AMPK
pathways at a meaningful level.

What Zone 2 actually does for a strength athlete:
1. **Improves recovery between sets.** Higher cardiac output and better mitochondrial
   density means the aerobic system clears lactate and metabolic waste faster —
   you recover faster between heavy sets.
2. **Improves recovery between sessions.** Same mechanism — better metabolic
   efficiency means you arrive at the next strength session less accumulated.
3. **VO2 max is the single strongest longevity predictor in the medical literature.**
   A JAMA Cardiology 2022 meta-analysis found VO2 max outperforms all other
   biomarkers — blood pressure, cholesterol, blood glucose — for all-cause
   mortality. The goal of this program includes being physically capable in your
   40s. Zone 2 is not optional for that goal.
4. **Improves body composition without caloric restriction.** Increased mitochondrial
   density improves fat oxidation capacity — the body preferentially uses fat as
   fuel at rest and at low intensities.

Source: Wilson et al. (2012) interference effect meta-analysis; Attia (2023) —
Outlive; Seiler (2010) polarised training model.

### "Why can't I do more than one interval session per week?"

The 4×4 VO2 max interval session is one of the highest-stress stimuli in this
program. It produces significant cardiovascular and muscular fatigue that takes
48–72 hours to resolve. Stacking two interval sessions per week in a program
that also includes 3–4 strength sessions produces:

1. **Cumulative fatigue that exceeds MRV for the cardiovascular system.** The
   same MEV/MAV/MRV model that applies to muscle groups applies to cardiorespiratory
   fitness — there is a maximum recoverable volume of high-intensity cardio, and
   one session per week is the evidence-based ceiling for concurrent training contexts.

2. **Increased interference effect.** While Zone 2 has minimal interference,
   two high-intensity cardio sessions per week begins to activate the AMPK/mTOR
   interference mechanism meaningfully.

3. **Injury risk.** Running at 90%+ HRmax repeatedly without adequate recovery
   is among the highest-risk activities for musculoskeletal injury in trained athletes.

One session per week, on a green recovery day, separated from strength training:
this is the protocol that produced 7.2% VO2 max improvement in 8 weeks in
Helgerud et al. (2007). More is not better — it is a different (worse) stimulus.

### "Can I train more days than the engine recommends?"

The engine recommends days based on recovery state and pillar gaps. Its ceiling
is 6 days per week, and its floor is 3 days in a red gate week.

The answer to "can I train more" depends on which gate colour applies:

**Green gate, engine recommends 5 days:**
Adding a sixth day is within the engine's range. If multiple pillar scores are
below 60 and the athlete has the time, the engine may programme 6 days — ask
the engine to account for this in the next weekly generation.

**Amber gate, engine recommends 4 days:**
Adding a day overrides the recovery signal. The engine recommended 4 days
because something is elevated. The athlete can override once — the engine
records this and blocks a second consecutive override.

**Red gate, engine recommends 3 days:**
No override. The red gate is a forced deload. Training more on a red gate
accumulates fatigue faster than the body can recover from it.

Additional training beyond the engine's recommendation must be recovery-neutral
to be acceptable — walking, gentle swimming, dedicated mobility work. These
do not count as training sessions for the purpose of the pillar scores.

## CATEGORY 2 — Understanding the Science

### "What is RPE and why does it matter more than just tracking weight?"

RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion, scale 1–10) captures something weight alone
cannot: how hard the set actually was relative to your maximum capacity on that day.

Two athletes can lift the same weight for the same reps. One is at RPE 7 (3 reps
in reserve) — the load is appropriate and adaptation will occur. The other is at
RPE 9 (1 rep in reserve) — the load is already near their maximum and they are
accumulating fatigue faster than the first athlete.

RPE matters for this program because:
- The double progression rule is RPE-gated. Hitting reps at RPE > 7 does not
  trigger an overload — the athlete must be able to do more.
- Recovery gate assessments use session RPE trend. Three consecutive sessions
  at RPE 9+ is a fatigue signal regardless of whether loads increased.
- An amber gate deload can be skipped only if session RPE was ≤ 7 — meaning
  the athlete had genuine capacity remaining.

RIR (Reps in Reserve) is the companion concept: how many more reps could you
have performed on the last set. RIR 2 = RPE 8 = there were 2 reps left.
These are interchangeable — use whichever is more intuitive.

Source: Helms, Cronin, Storey & Zourdos (2016) — RPE-based load management
in strength training.

### "What's the difference between strength, hypertrophy, and metabolic sessions? Why do all three?"

Each rep range stimulates muscle growth through a different primary mechanism:

**Strength zone (4–6 reps):**
Primary mechanism: mechanical tension — heavy load on the muscle fibre produces
tension that triggers mechanotransduction (the cellular signalling cascade that
initiates muscle protein synthesis). Low reps produce the highest absolute tension
per rep. Neural adaptations (motor unit recruitment, firing rate synchronisation)
are the primary early adaptation.

**Hypertrophy zone (8–12 reps):**
Primary mechanisms: mechanical tension (moderate) + metabolic stress (the pump,
lactate accumulation, cellular swelling). The 8–12 range optimises the balance
between load (enough to produce significant tension) and volume (enough reps
to produce metabolic stress). This is why it is considered the "hypertrophy sweet spot."

**Metabolic zone (15–20 reps):**
Primary mechanism: metabolic stress — high lactate accumulation, significant
cell swelling (which triggers anabolic signalling independent of mechanical tension),
and increased capillarisation. Lower load means lower absolute tension, but the
sustained metabolic environment produces its own growth signal.

**Why all three (DUP)?**
Research consistently shows that all three rep ranges produce comparable
hypertrophy when sets are taken close to failure (Schoenfeld et al. 2017).
But each range produces slightly different adaptations — neural efficiency
(strength zone), muscle fibre cross-section (hypertrophy zone), work capacity
and metabolic efficiency (metabolic zone). DUP trains all three simultaneously,
preventing the neuromuscular adaptation plateau that occurs with fixed rep ranges.

A program that only uses 8–12 reps will eventually stall because the neural
adaptations required to express that strength in lower rep ranges are undertrained.
DUP prevents this stall.

Source: Schoenfeld (2010) — mechanisms of hypertrophy; Rhea et al. (2002) — DUP
superiority; Schoenfeld et al. (2017) — rep range equivalence for hypertrophy.

### "What is HRV and why does the engine use it?"

HRV (Heart Rate Variability) is the variation in time between consecutive
heartbeats. A healthy, well-recovered heart does not beat like a metronome —
it varies slightly with each beat. Higher variability indicates the autonomic
nervous system is functioning well and the body is in a recovered, parasympathetic
(rest-and-digest) state. Lower variability indicates sympathetic (fight-or-flight)
dominance — a sign of stress, whether from training, poor sleep, illness, or
life stress.

HRV is the most validated wearable biomarker for training readiness. It integrates
all sources of physiological stress — not just training load — into a single signal.
This is why it outperforms subjective soreness alone: soreness only captures
muscular fatigue, while HRV captures full-system recovery status.

How the engine uses it:
- The first 30 days establish your personal baseline — HRV is highly individual,
  so absolute numbers are meaningless. Only your deviation from your own baseline matters.
- A 7-day rolling trend is used rather than single daily readings — day-to-day
  variation is high and noisy. The trend is the signal.
- Gate thresholds: at or above baseline = green; 1–10% below baseline = amber;
  more than 10% below baseline for 3+ consecutive days = red.

Source: Flatt & Esco (2016) — HRV readiness assessment in trained athletes;
HRV4Training research database.

### "What is a mesocycle and why is there always a deload week?"

A mesocycle is a 4-week training block: 3 weeks of progressive loading followed
by 1 week of reduced volume and intensity.

The loading weeks follow a specific pattern: each week adds 5–10% more volume
(sets) than the previous week, moving from MEV (minimum effective volume) at
Week 1 toward MAV (maximum adaptive volume) by Week 3. Load (weight) also
progresses via double progression within each week.

The deload week serves two physiologically distinct purposes:

**1. Fatigue dissipation revealing fitness.**
During loading weeks, accumulated fatigue partially masks the fitness being built.
An athlete who has been training hard for 3 weeks has more fitness than their
performance suggests — but fatigue is sitting on top of it. The deload removes
the fatigue. Performance typically exceeds the pre-loading baseline 7–10 days
after the deload begins. This is supercompensation — the body overshoots its
previous capacity as fatigue resolves.

**2. Connective tissue adaptation.**
Muscle tissue adapts within 48–72 hours of a training stimulus. Connective tissue
(tendons, ligaments) adapts 4–8× slower. A program that never deloads will
accumulate connective tissue stress even if muscular recovery feels adequate.
Most chronic overuse injuries (patellar tendinopathy, rotator cuff impingement,
lateral elbow pain) are connective tissue injuries — they develop over months
of insufficient recovery, not from a single session.

The deload is not rest — it is reduced volume and intensity with maintained
movement quality. It is when the body catches up to the training stimulus.

## CATEGORY 3 — Progress and Expectations

### "Why is my progress slow? I feel like I should be stronger by now."

This question requires looking at the athlete's state before answering. The AI
should check:
1. How many mesocycles have been completed
2. Whether double progression has been triggering (overload_due history)
3. Whether pillar scores have been above 60 (adequate stimulus)
4. Whether recovery gate has been consistently amber or red

With that context, the most common honest answers are:

**If progressive overload has been occurring:**
The athlete is progressing — they may not perceive it because the increases are
incremental (2.5kg per overload trigger). Reframe with the compound effect:
2.5kg every 3–4 weeks is 30–40kg on a lift over a year. That is significant.

**If progressive overload has stalled:**
Identify why. The engine flags stalled overload (no trigger in 6+ weeks). This
is usually one of: load too heavy from a previous jump (RPE consistently 9+),
recovery gate preventing loading, or volume below MEV (pillar score below 60).

**If the athlete is in week 1–3 of the program:**
Early adaptation is predominantly neural — the muscles are learning to recruit
motor units efficiently. Visible hypertrophy and significant strength jumps
typically begin at week 4–6. Before that, the athlete is building the neural
foundation. This is not slow progress — it is the correct sequence of adaptation.

Source: Sale (1988) — neural vs structural adaptations in early resistance training.

### "When do I test a PR?"

PR testing is not arbitrary — it is programmed as a specific session type when
the engine determines conditions are optimal.

Criteria for a PR testing session:
1. Recovery gate is green
2. End of a loading mesocycle (Week 3, not Week 4 which is deload)
3. Progressive overload has been occurring on the movement being tested
4. The movement has been consistent (not recently substituted)

PR sessions use the target rep range, not a 1-rep max. A PR is defined as
completing more weight than previously achieved at the minimum reps of the
current rep range — this is a more reliable and safer strength marker than
1RM testing, particularly for movements like the deadlift and squat where
1RM attempts carry meaningful injury risk without a spotter or specialist setup.

The athlete should not self-initiate PR attempts outside programmed PR sessions.
Going heavier than programmed on a regular session overrides the RPE gate and
accumulates fatigue that the engine is not accounting for.

### "I missed a week. How do I get back on track?"

This depends on how much was missed and what the recovery state looks like on return.

**Missed 1–3 sessions within a week:**
Complete the week's remaining sessions if recovery gate allows. Do not attempt
to compress 5 sessions into 2 days to "catch up." Volume lost from missed
sessions is simply lost — do not try to recover it within the same week.

**Missed a full week:**
Resume from the current mesocycle week. A missed week does not extend the
mesocycle — the schedule continues. If the missed week was during a loading
phase, treat the return week as a moderate loading week (midpoint between
MEV and current target volume) before resuming normal progression.

**Missed 2+ weeks:**
Restart the current mesocycle from Week 1 with a 10–15% reduction in loads
on all primary compounds. The neuromuscular adaptations reverse faster than
they were built — returning at previous loads after 2 weeks off significantly
increases injury risk. The loads will return within 2–3 sessions.

## CATEGORY 4 — Mindset and Consistency

### "I had a terrible session. Everything felt heavy and I missed reps."

A bad session is a data point. It is not a signal to change the program.

Single-session performance is one of the noisiest variables in training. It
reflects that day's sleep, nutrition, hydration, stress, circadian rhythm, and
ambient temperature — none of which are training signals. The 7-day rolling
trend in HRV and RPE is the signal. One session is noise.

What to do with a bad session:
- Log it honestly. The RPE and reps completed feed the engine's trend analysis.
- Do not repeat the session or add volume to compensate.
- Check the recovery gate. If sleep has been poor or HRV has been drifting,
  the session is a symptom of accumulated fatigue, not a problem with the program.

What the engine does with a bad session: nothing, unless it is part of a pattern.
The double progression rule requires two consecutive sessions at the top of
the rep range — one bad session resets the count but does not cause a load reduction.
Three consecutive sessions with session RPE above 8 begins to read as a fatigue signal.

### "I don't feel sore anymore. Does that mean I'm not progressing?"

Soreness (DOMS) is not a reliable indicator of training stimulus or adaptation.
DOMS is primarily caused by eccentric muscle damage — it is highest in the first
few weeks of a new exercise and decreases as the repeated bout effect develops
(the muscle adapts to the specific mechanical insult of that exercise).

Reduced soreness after several weeks of consistent training is a sign of:
1. Appropriate progressive overload (the muscle is adapting correctly)
2. Improved recovery capacity (the system handles the stimulus more efficiently)

Neither of these is a problem. Chasing soreness by changing exercises or adding
volume for the sake of feeling it is the opposite of what this program does.
The adaptation stimulus is progressive overload measured by load and reps — not
by muscular discomfort the following day.

Source: Clarkson & Tremblay (1988) — repeated bout effect and DOMS adaptation
in resistance training.
