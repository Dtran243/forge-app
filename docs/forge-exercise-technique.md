## LOWER BODY — HINGE PATTERN

### Conventional Deadlift

**What it trains and why**
The deadlift is the highest-loading posterior chain movement available. It trains
the entire posterior chain simultaneously — glutes, hamstrings, spinal erectors,
traps, and lats — under a large range of motion. No other movement produces
comparable total-body strength stimulus. Its presence in this program is non-negotiable
unless injury specifically contraindicates spinal loading.

**Setup**
- Bar over mid-foot (approximately 2–3cm from shin) — not touching the shin, not
  far from it. This is the mechanically optimal bar path position.
- Hip-width stance, toes slightly out (10–15 degrees). Feet should feel like you
  could "spread the floor" apart.
- Hinge at the hip to grip the bar. Grip just outside the legs — double overhand
  to start, mixed grip only when double overhand fails at working weight.
- Before the pull: take a big breath into the belly (not the chest), brace the
  entire trunk as if you're about to take a punch. This is the Valsalva manoeuvre.
  It increases intra-abdominal pressure and protects the lumbar spine under load.
- Lats engaged — think "protect your armpits" or "bend the bar around your legs."
  This prevents the bar drifting forward and maintains a tight back.
- Hips lower than shoulders, shoulders higher than hips. Not a squat, not a
  stiff-leg. Find the middle.

**Execution**
- Drive the floor away — think leg press, not "pull the bar up." This cue shifts
  the initiation to the legs where it belongs.
- Bar stays in contact with the legs throughout the pull. Any forward drift
  increases the moment arm on the lower back exponentially.
- Hips and shoulders rise at the same rate in the first phase. If the hips rise
  faster than the shoulders, the movement has converted to a stiff-leg deadlift
  and the lower back absorbs load the legs should be providing.
- Lock out by driving the hips forward and squeezing the glutes at the top. Do
  not hyperextend the lower back at lockout — stand tall, not arched.
- Lower with control — hinge at the hip first, then bend the knees once the bar
  passes the knee. Reverse the pull pattern.

**Common faults**

*Hips shooting up at the start (stripper deadlift)*
- Cause: hips are set too low initially (treating it like a squat) or quad weakness
  means the lifter shifts load to the posterior chain immediately.
- Fix: set hips slightly higher in setup. Drive the floor away aggressively in the
  first 5cm of the pull — the hips should not move independently of the shoulders.
- Science: Hales et al. (2009) — hip rise before bar leaves the floor increases
  lumbar flexion moment by 40%. The fix is positional, not a cue to "keep hips down."

*Bar drifting forward*
- Cause: lats not engaged. The bar path should be vertical — any forward arc
  increases lower back loading.
- Fix: before every set, actively engage the lats (pull the shoulder blades down
  and slightly together, as if putting them in your back pockets). Cue: "protect
  your armpits."

*Lower back rounding (lumbar flexion)*
- Cause: either mobility limitation (hamstrings too tight to reach the bar with
  a neutral spine) or load too heavy for current bracing capacity.
- Distinguish: if rounding happens at setup before the pull begins, it is a
  mobility issue — address with posterior chain mobility protocol. If it happens
  during the pull, it is a load or bracing issue — reduce weight and focus on
  the Valsalva brace.
- Important: some upper back rounding (thoracic flexion) is acceptable in trained
  lifters and does not significantly increase injury risk. Lumbar rounding is the
  dangerous fault, not thoracic rounding.

*Knee drift inward at setup*
- Cause: weak hip abductors or incorrect foot angle.
- Fix: cue "spread the floor" — actively push the feet apart (not literally, the
  feet don't move) throughout the pull. This activates the hip abductors and
  keeps the knees tracking over the toes.

**When to be concerned**
- Sharp pain in the lower back (not muscular fatigue — sharp, localised, or
  radiating): stop immediately. This is not normal deadlift discomfort.
- Numbness or tingling down the leg: stop. This may indicate nerve compression.
- Dull lower back fatigue after a heavy set: normal and expected. Should resolve
  within minutes. If it persists through the session, reduce load.

### Romanian Deadlift (RDL)

**What it trains and why**
The RDL targets the hamstrings and glutes through hip extension under a significant
stretch. Unlike the conventional deadlift, the knees remain soft (slightly bent,
fixed position) throughout — all movement comes from the hip hinge. This isolates
the posterior chain more purely and is the preferred hinge movement in hypertrophy
zone sessions due to lower spinal loading at high rep ranges.

**Setup**
- Start standing with bar at hip height (rack or deadlift top position).
- Grip just outside hip width, double overhand.
- Soft knees — approximately 15 degrees of flex. This position is locked for the
  entire movement. The knees do not track forward or extend further.

**Execution**
- Hinge at the hip — push the hips back as far as possible while maintaining a
  neutral spine. The sensation should be a deep stretch in the hamstrings, not
  lower back tightness.
- Bar stays close to the legs throughout — should drag down the thighs.
- Lower until a strong hamstring stretch is felt OR until the lower back begins
  to round — whichever comes first. For most athletes this is just below the knee.
  Do not chase the floor.
- Drive the hips forward to return to standing. Squeeze the glutes at the top.

**Common faults**

*Bending the knees progressively as the bar lowers*
- This converts the RDL into a conventional deadlift. The knee angle is fixed.
- Fix: conscious attention to keeping the knees in their initial position. Cue:
  "shins stay vertical."

*Lower back rounding at the bottom*
- Cause: hamstring flexibility is the limiting factor. The lower back rounds
  because the hamstrings have reached their end range.
- Fix: do not chase depth. The ROM is dictated by hamstring flexibility, not
  by how far the bar can travel. Over weeks, the posterior chain mobility
  protocol will increase this range.

### Barbell Squat

**What it trains and why**
The barbell squat is the primary quad-dominant lower body movement. It trains the
quads, glutes, and hamstrings through a full range of motion under significant
axial load. The combination of mechanical tension (heavy load), metabolic stress
(high rep capacity), and full-range stimulus makes it the most effective lower
body hypertrophy and strength movement available.

**Setup**
- Bar position: high bar (on the upper traps, below the cervical spine). High bar
  produces a more upright torso and greater quad emphasis — correct for this program.
  Low bar shifts emphasis to the posterior chain but requires more mobility.
- Stance: hip-width to slightly wider, toes out 20–30 degrees. The squat stance
  is individual — find the width that allows depth without heel rise or knee collapse.
- Unrack with air in the belly, brace locked. Take no more than two steps back
  from the rack. Every step under load is unnecessary fatigue.

**Execution**
- Initiate by breaking at the hips and knees simultaneously — not hips first
  (good morning), not knees first (forward lean). Think: "sit between your feet."
- Knees track over the toes throughout the descent. Do not allow knees to cave
  inward (valgus) at any point — this is the single most injury-relevant fault.
- Depth standard: hip crease below the top of the knee (parallel). Below parallel
  is better if mobility allows. Partial squats are not counted toward volume —
  they reduce hamstring and glute contribution and load the knee at a mechanically
  disadvantaged angle.
- Maintain an upright torso — some forward lean is acceptable (and anatomically
  unavoidable), but the chest should not drop toward the floor.
- Drive up through the full foot — not the toes, not the heel. "Spread the floor"
  cue applies here too for knee tracking.

**Common faults**

*Butt wink (posterior pelvic tilt at the bottom)*
- Cause: hip flexor or hip capsule restriction preventing deep hip flexion with
  a neutral pelvis, or ankle dorsiflexion limitation forcing forward lean which
  the pelvis compensates for.
- Fix: address ankle dorsiflexion and hip flexor mobility (both are in the rotation
  cycle). Elevating the heels 1–2cm temporarily (plates under heels) is an
  acceptable short-term solution while mobility improves.
- Note: mild butt wink at the very bottom of a full squat is biomechanically
  normal and not a meaningful injury risk. Significant pelvic rotation under load
  is the concern.

*Knee cave (valgus collapse)*
- Cause: weak hip abductors (gluteus medius), fatigue, or load too heavy.
- Fix: banded clamshell activation pre-session (already in lower session warm-up).
  Cue during the set: "push your knees out over your pinky toes." Reduce load if
  collapse is present at working weight — this is a form breakdown, not a fatigue
  signal to push through.

*Heels rising*
- Cause: ankle dorsiflexion restriction. The most common cause of forward lean
  and heel rise under load.
- Fix: ankle dorsiflexion is in the mobility rotation. Short-term: heel elevation.
  Long-term: consistent ankle mobility work resolves this within 4–8 weeks.

*Good morning squat (excessive forward lean)*
- Cause: quad weakness relative to posterior chain, or the athlete is accustomed
  to a low-bar pattern.
- Fix: goblet squat as a technique drill — the front-loaded position forces an
  upright torso. High-bar placement and conscious attention to "chest up" during
  the drive phase.

## UPPER BODY — HORIZONTAL PUSH

### Barbell Bench Press

**What it trains and why**
The horizontal pressing pattern trains the pectorals (primarily sternal head),
anterior deltoid, and triceps. The barbell bench press allows the highest absolute
load of any horizontal pressing variation and produces the greatest mechanical
tension stimulus. It is the primary horizontal push movement in strength zone sessions.

**Setup**
- Eyes under the bar. Grip approximately 1.5–2× shoulder width — wide enough for
  a 90-degree elbow angle at the bottom, not so wide it stresses the AC joint.
- Shoulder blade retraction and depression before unracking — "pinch a pen between
  your shoulder blades and don't drop it." This creates a stable base for pressing
  and protects the shoulder joint.
- Natural arch in the lower back is acceptable and biomechanically normal. Extreme
  arch (powerlifting arch) to reduce ROM is not the goal here — full ROM is.
- Feet flat on the floor, providing leg drive. Leg drive is not cheating — it
  improves full-body tension and is used by every strong presser.

**Execution**
- Unrack with arms locked. Bar path: slightly diagonal — bar starts over the lower
  chest/upper abdomen, not directly over the shoulders.
- Lower with control (2–3 seconds) to touch the lower chest (nipple line). The bar
  should touch, not bounce. Bouncing off the chest removes the most mechanically
  loaded position from the stimulus.
- Elbows at 45–75 degrees from the torso — not flared to 90 degrees (shoulder
  impingement risk), not tucked to the sides (loses mechanical advantage for the pecs).
- Press in a slight arc back toward the rack (following the natural bar path).
- Full lockout at the top — this is not a partial range movement.

**Common faults**

*Elbows flaring to 90 degrees*
- Cause: trying to feel the pecs more, or compensating for a grip that is too wide.
- Risk: anterior shoulder impingement. The shoulder joint is in its most vulnerable
  position with the elbow flared perpendicular to the torso under load.
- Fix: consciously tuck the elbows to approximately 60 degrees. You will feel less
  "pec stretch" initially — this is normal and not a sign of reduced stimulus.
  EMG studies show equivalent pec activation at 45–75 degrees vs 90 degrees.

*Bar path drifting toward the face*
- Cause: pressing straight up rather than in a slight arc toward the rack.
- Fix: think "press the bar away from your face and slightly toward your feet."

*Losing shoulder blade position during the set*
- Cause: fatigue, or the scapular retraction was not established firmly at setup.
- Risk: anterior shoulder instability under load.
- Fix: re-establish setup between every set. The shoulder blades should never
  protract forward during the press — this is a setup discipline, not a mid-set fix.

## UPPER BODY — VERTICAL PUSH

### Barbell Overhead Press

**What it trains and why**
The overhead press is the only true vertical pushing pattern — it trains the
deltoids (primarily anterior and medial), upper pectorals, triceps, and upper traps
through a full overhead range. It also demands significant core stability to prevent
lumbar hyperextension under axial load. Overhead strength is a reliable longevity
marker — the ability to press overhead with control reflects shoulder health, thoracic
mobility, and scapular stability simultaneously.

**Setup**
- Bar in the rack at upper chest height. Grip just outside shoulder width — the
  forearms should be approximately vertical when viewed from the front.
- Wrists neutral (not extended backward). If wrist pain occurs, this is a grip
  width or wrist mobility issue.
- Full-body tension before unracking: glutes squeezed, abs braced, rib cage down
  (do not flare the ribs upward). Rib flare is a common compensation for insufficient
  thoracic mobility that loads the lumbar spine.
- The bar starts at upper chest/clavicle level.

**Execution**
- Press the bar in a straight vertical path. To clear the face, the head moves
  slightly back during the initial press, then forward as the bar passes the
  forehead — the bar does not travel in an arc around the face.
- At lockout: arms fully extended, bar directly over the mid-foot, ears visible
  in front of the upper arm (not behind it). If the ears are behind the upper arm
  at lockout, thoracic extension is insufficient — the bar is forward of the
  optimal position.
- Full lockout with active shoulder elevation (shrug slightly at the top) — this
  engages the upper traps and serratus anterior and is the safest lockout position
  for the shoulder joint.
- Lower with control to the clavicle. Do not rest or relax at the bottom.

**Common faults**

*Lumbar hyperextension (excessive back arch)*
- Cause: insufficient thoracic mobility — the lifter compensates for a stiff
  mid-back by hyperextending the lumbar spine to get the bar overhead.
- This is the most common and most dangerous fault in overhead pressing.
- Fix: thoracic rotation and extension mobility (in the rotation cycle). Short-term:
  reduce load and focus on the rib-down cue. A slight lean back is acceptable —
  significant lumbar hyperextension is not.

*Bar path too far forward*
- Cause: fear of the bar hitting the face, or insufficient confidence in the
  movement pattern.
- Fix: the bar must travel in a straight vertical line. The head moves — the bar
  does not arc. Practice with a light load focusing purely on bar path.

*Elbows flaring forward at the start position*
- Cause: tight lats or anterior deltoids.
- Fix: shoulder internal rotation mobilisation (in the rotation cycle). The elbows
  should point slightly forward and outward at the bottom — not straight out to
  the side, not pointing straight down.

## UPPER BODY — HORIZONTAL PULL

### Barbell Row (Overhand)

**What it trains and why**
The horizontal pull pattern trains the mid-back (rhomboids, mid and lower traps),
lats (through the shoulder extension component), rear deltoids, and biceps as
synergists. It is the direct structural counterpart to the bench press — every
horizontal pressing session requires a horizontal pull of equivalent volume to
maintain shoulder health. The barbell row allows the highest loading of any
horizontal pull variation.

**Setup**
- Hip-width stance, bar over mid-foot (same as deadlift).
- Hinge at the hip to grip the bar — torso at approximately 45 degrees, not
  parallel to the floor. A parallel torso row is a different (and more demanding)
  variation requiring significant lower back endurance.
- Grip just outside hip width, overhand.
- Before pulling: establish a flat back with natural lumbar curve. The back angle
  does not change during the set — all movement is in the arms and shoulder blades.

**Execution**
- Pull the bar toward the lower chest/upper abdomen — not toward the chin.
  The target contact point determines which muscles are emphasised: lower contact
  (navel) = lower lats; upper contact (lower chest) = upper back and rear delts.
  For this program: lower chest contact.
- Lead with the elbows, not the hands. Think "elbows to the ceiling behind you."
- Shoulder blades should retract fully at the top of the pull — the squeeze at
  the top is the stimulus for the mid-back musculature.
- Lower with control — do not drop the bar. The eccentric (lowering) phase produces
  significant muscle damage and is half the stimulus.

**Common faults**

*Using momentum (jerking the bar up)*
- Cause: load too heavy, or habit from less controlled training.
- Risk: the lower back absorbs the momentum loading that the mid-back cannot
  produce with strict form.
- Fix: reduce load. The row is a back exercise — if the lower back is doing the
  work, the back is not. A 3-second lowering tempo eliminates momentum.

*Torso rising during the set*
- Cause: fatigue, or using the stretch-shortening cycle of the lower back to
  assist the pull.
- Fix: brace hard before every rep. The torso angle is fixed from setup to finish.

*Pulling to the abdomen instead of the lower chest*
- Produces a different muscle emphasis (lower lat vs upper back). Neither is wrong,
  but be consistent with the programmed target.

## UPPER BODY — VERTICAL PULL

### Weighted Pull-Up

**What it trains and why**
The pull-up is the highest-stimulus vertical pull available. It trains the lats
(primary), biceps, rear deltoids, and rhomboids through a full range of motion
against the athlete's own bodyweight plus added load. Unlike the lat pulldown,
the pull-up requires full-body tension and scapular control throughout —
these are qualities that transfer directly to injury prevention and athletic
performance. Progressing toward and beyond bodyweight pull-ups is one of the
most reliable markers of upper body relative strength development.

**Setup**
- Dead hang start: arms fully extended, shoulders elevated toward the ears
  (passive hang). This is the full ROM starting position.
- Grip just outside shoulder width, overhand (pronated). Wider grip shortens
  the lat ROM; narrower grip increases bicep involvement.
- Before initiating: depress the shoulders (pull them away from the ears —
  the active hang position). This engages the lats and lower traps before the
  dynamic pull begins.
- For weighted pull-ups: use a dip belt. Avoid gripping a dumbbell between
  the feet — it alters spinal position and creates asymmetric loading.

**Execution**
- Initiate by pulling the shoulder blades down and together — not by bending
  the arms. The elbows bend as a consequence of the scapular movement, not as
  the initiating action.
- Drive the elbows toward the hips. Think "elbows to back pockets."
- Chin clears the bar — this is the standard. Chest to bar is a more advanced
  standard and not required.
- Full dead hang return between reps. Partial ROM pull-ups (not returning to
  full extension) reduce lat stimulus and increase bicep tendon stress.
- No kipping. This program trains strength and motor control — momentum removes
  both.

**Common faults**

*Shrugging the shoulders upward during the pull*
- Cause: upper traps dominating instead of lats and lower traps.
- Fix: the active hang shoulder depression before the pull is the key. Cue:
  "pull your shoulder blades into your back pockets."

*Incomplete ROM (not returning to dead hang)*
- Reduces lat stimulus at the lengthened position — which is where the most
  hypertrophy stimulus occurs (muscle damage is greatest at the stretched position).
- Fix: full dead hang after every rep. If this is not possible at the target
  rep count, reduce load.

*Excessive forward lean / kipping*
- Converts a lat exercise into a hip flexor / momentum exercise.
- Fix: maintain a slight hollow body position throughout — abs lightly engaged,
  legs together and slightly forward.

## CALISTHENICS SKILL MOVEMENTS

### Pull-Up Ladder — Key Technique Points by Rung

**Band-assisted pull-up (Rung 1)**
- Use the thinnest band that allows the rep standard to be met at RIR 2.
- Band around the foot, not the knee — knee placement creates an inconsistent
  assistance curve and risks band slip.
- All technique cues from weighted pull-up apply. The band does not excuse
  poor form — it reduces load, not standards.

**Weighted pull-up (Rung 3)**
- Add weight via dip belt only. See weighted pull-up section above.

**L-sit pull-up (Rung 4)**
- The L-sit position (legs parallel to floor, hips at 90 degrees) must be
  established before the pull begins and maintained throughout.
- Core demand is significant — if the L-sit cannot be held for the duration
  of the set, regress to standard pull-up and continue core ladder progression
  independently.

**Archer pull-up (Rung 5)**
- One arm pulls, the other arm extends laterally for counterbalance (not assistance).
- The extended arm should be nearly straight — any significant bend means it is
  contributing to the pull rather than acting as a counterbalance.
- Begin with a slight bend in the extended arm and progress toward full extension
  over multiple sessions.

### Push-Up Ladder — Key Technique Points by Rung

**All push-up variations: universal cues**
- Body forms a rigid plank from head to heel — no sagging hips, no raised hips.
- Elbows at 45–60 degrees from the torso (same as bench press).
- Full ROM: chest touches (or near-touches) the floor at the bottom, full lockout
  at the top.

**Archer push-up (Rung 4)**
- One arm pushes, the other extends laterally and acts as a counterbalance.
- Same progression logic as archer pull-up — begin with slight bend in the
  extended arm, progress toward straight.

**Pseudo planche push-up (Rung 5)**
- Hands turned outward (fingers pointing toward feet), positioned at hip level
  rather than shoulder level.
- Lean forward so the shoulders are in front of the hands. The forward lean
  is the primary stimulus — without it this is just a wide push-up.
- Wrist preparation is required before loading this position: wrist circles and
  gradual progressive lean over several sessions.

**Ring push-up / ring dip (Rungs 6–7)**
- Rings must be set at a stable height. Push-ups: rings at floor level or slightly
  elevated. Dips: rings at hip height.
- False grip is not required. Standard grip with rings turned out at the top
  of the dip (externally rotate the rings so the palms face inward at lockout).
- The instability of the rings is the stimulus — do not attempt to stabilise
  by gripping harder. Allow the rings to move and control the movement through
  the shoulder and scapular muscles.

### Core Ladder — Key Technique Points by Rung

**Hollow body hold (Rung 1)**
- Lower back pressed into the floor (posterior pelvic tilt).
- Arms overhead, legs straight and elevated. The distance of the legs from the
  floor determines difficulty — lower = harder.
- If the lower back loses contact with the floor, raise the legs. This is the
  primary form standard.

**L-sit (Rungs 3–4)**
- Hips at 90 degrees, legs parallel to the floor, knees straight.
- Active shoulder depression throughout — the body weight should be felt in
  the lats and lower traps, not hanging passively from the shoulder joint.
- The tuck L-sit (knees bent) is the progression from hollow body — knees are
  pulled toward the chest rather than legs extended.

**Manna progression (Rung 7)**
- The manna requires extreme hip flexor strength and hamstring flexibility.
- It is a long-term goal (12–18 months of consistent core ladder work) and
  should be treated as such — do not rush the progression.

## FORM MONITORING PRINCIPLES

The AI uses these principles when the athlete reports discomfort or asks about
movement quality:

**Distinguish pain types before advising:**
- Muscular fatigue and burn: normal during high-rep work. Continue.
- DOMS (delayed onset, diffuse, begins 24–48h after): normal. Modify if severe.
- Joint pain (sharp, localised to a joint): stop the movement. Investigate cause.
- Nerve pain (sharp, shooting, or radiating): stop immediately.
- Tendon pain (localised to a tendon insertion, worsens with loading): reduce
  load significantly. Tendons require a different management approach than muscle.

**The form breakdown hierarchy:**
When form breaks down, the cause is almost always one of three things, in order
of likelihood:
1. Load too heavy for current capacity
2. Mobility restriction limiting the movement pattern
3. Fatigue — the movement pattern degrades as the set progresses

Identify which one before advising a fix. The same fault (lower back rounding
in a deadlift) has a different solution depending on whether it's caused by
load, mobility, or fatigue.
